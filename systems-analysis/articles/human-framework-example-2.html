The Peloponnesian War, 431 – 404 BCE — Athens and its maritime empire

Summary: The leading naval and imperial power of Greece — a radical direct democracy ruling a maritime empire of tribute-paying allied cities — tested to destruction across a 27-year war against Sparta and its alliance, and finally defeated in 404 BCE. The single factor that organizes the whole arc is Athens's total dependence on command of the sea: the empire, the war-chest, the imported grain that fed the city, and Pericles's entire strategy of outlasting Sparta from behind the Long Walls all rested on holding the sea lanes. That dependence was Athens's strength for as long as it held and its one fatal exposure once Persian money built a Spartan fleet able to break it — the loss of the navy at Aegospotami in 405 BCE and the grain blockade that followed ended the war within a year. The margin that might have absorbed the blow had already been spent on the self-inflicted catastrophe of the Sicilian Expedition, 415–413 BCE.

D01. Physical Domain

A few miles inland in Attica, on dry rocky soil poor for grain — olives and vines, not bread — so the city always depended on imported grain by sea. Its physical assets: the silver mines at Laurion in southern Attica that had paid for the fleet, the great port of Piraeus, and the Long Walls — the fortified corridor joining city to port that let Athens be fed by sea while Sparta held the land. Pericles's plan packed the whole population behind those walls and gave up the countryside; Sparta's near-yearly invasions cut Attica's slow-growing orchards, and from 413 BCE a permanent Spartan fort at Decelea made the occupation continuous and severed the city from the mines. Residue: a countryside stripped of its orchards, an enemy garrison fixed in Attica, and at the end the Long Walls pulled down by the victors — wear left in the land that did not clear.

D02. Biological Domain

The plague is the clearest case in the whole war of biology deciding events. Packing the rural population inside the walls created lethal crowding, and from 430 BCE an epidemic tore through the city for several years, killing perhaps a quarter to a third of Athenians — Pericles among them in 429 BCE — with ancient medicine helpless against it. Beyond the plague, citizen manpower was the binding limit: every rower and hoplite lost to disease, to the army destroyed at Sicily, and to steady attrition came from a finite pool Athens could not refill fast enough. Residue: a permanent demographic wound — a generation of men gone to plague and to Sicily, the citizen body smaller at the war's end than at its start, and no capacity to heal what was lost.

D03. Economic Domain

Athens's claimed edge was money — it could outspend and outlast Sparta. It rested on the empire's tribute, silver paid yearly by allied cities, plus the Laurion mines and the trade through Piraeus, the busiest port in Greece, which built the largest war-chest in the Greek world and a reserve held on the Acropolis. The system ran on the sea: the grain that fed the city came from the Black Sea through the narrow Hellespont, so provisioning hung on one supply line. The base eroded as the war dragged — the reserve drained, the Sicilian Expedition sank a fortune for nothing, Decelea cut the mines off and some twenty thousand skilled slaves deserted, revolts shrank the tribute, and Athens fell back on a harbor tax and finally melted sacred treasures, just as Persian gold built Sparta a fleet. Residue: a squandered reserve, an exhausted treasury, the lost mines — the financial advantage Athens had counted on, spent and broken.

D04. Social Domain

A radical direct democracy — decisions by the citizen Assembly, a Council of 500, and large popular courts, offices filled by lot. The citizen body was layered: a propertied elite of cavalry and hoplites, and the poorer thetes who rowed the fleet and formed the democracy's base; outside it stood the metics (resident foreigners who ran much of the trade, with no vote) and a large enslaved population. Power abroad ran through the navy and the empire's machinery of tribute, garrisons, and settlements. Two strains showed under the war: the empire was rule by force over resentful allies who revolted as Athens weakened; and the propertied and the poor split until, in 411 BCE, oligarchs briefly overthrew the democracy — though the fleet at Samos stayed loyal to it and it was restored. Residue: the empire's accumulated grievance, the class split surfacing as coups, and finally the destruction of the whole apparatus — empire dissolved, democracy overthrown by the Spartan-backed Thirty, a narrow and brutal oligarchy.

D05. Emotional Domain

How well Athens judged its own decisions mattered as much as any material factor, and that judgment grew erratic. Pericles gave steady strategic sense at the start; after his death in 429 BCE, leadership passed to more volatile figures — Cleon, then others — and a swayable Assembly that increasingly decided by mood. Its temper swung between overconfidence and panic: the Sicilian Expedition rode a wave of enthusiasm that overrode caution, and after disasters the city turned on itself — most self-destructively in 406 BCE, when the Assembly in a fury executed six of its own victorious generals for failing to rescue survivors after a battle. Trust frayed badly: the mutilation of sacred statues in 415 BCE set off a paranoid hunt for traitors, and Alcibiades's defection to Sparta deepened it. Residue: the exhaustion and bitterness of the late war, broken internal trust, and the demoralization of repeated defeat — the failure of nerve the Thirty would later exploit.

D06. Informational Domain

Athens decided in the open: the Assembly was the forum where policy was argued and set by persuasion, and news crossed the empire by ship. The culture was oral and rhetorical — the great public speeches, and a theater that engaged the war directly, Aristophanes's comedies openly mocking leaders and pushing for peace. Open debate was a strength but left the city at the mercy of whoever could move a crowd, and of bad information: the Sicilian Expedition was launched partly on false, overoptimistic reports of Sicilian wealth and weakness — a costly intelligence failure. The same openness could tip into frenzy, like the wave of denunciations over the mutilated statues in 415 BCE. Residue: the distortion behind the worst decisions — the false picture of Sicily, the inflamed rhetoric, the rumor and informing of 415 BCE — noise between reality and what the Assembly believed, never cleared in time to matter.

D07. Moral Domain

At home Athens prized law: popular courts, large citizen juries, written statutes, equality before the law among citizens, all held up as proof of its superiority. Abroad it ruled by force, and increasingly said so. Twice the gap is on record: in 427 BCE the Assembly voted to kill every man of rebellious Mytilene, reversed it a day later, and even then the deciding argument was expediency, not mercy; in 416 BCE, demanding the surrender of neutral Melos, Athenian envoys dropped justice entirely — the strong do what they can, the weak suffer what they must — and on refusal killed the men and enslaved the rest. The contradiction between the democracy's ideals at home and its conduct abroad is the war's central moral fact. Sanction at home could be lawless too, as in the illegal group trial of the generals in 406 BCE. Residue: the unpunished cruelties of empire and the gap between justice owed and given — a self-image permanently stained, and the unease that would help condemn Socrates in 399 BCE.

D08. Intellectual Domain

The paradox of the war: Athens collapsed politically at the height of its intellectual life. The sophists were teaching rhetoric and moral relativism, Socrates was at work, Sophocles and Euripides were writing through the war, and Thucydides was inventing critical history out of the conflict itself. Its practical edge was naval — Athenian crews were the most skilled seamen in Greece, and the empire rested on that superiority. The war partly turned on whether Sparta, with Persian money, could close the gap; under Lysander it finally did. The other loss was strategic: Pericles's coherent grand design gave way to the indiscipline that produced Sicily. Residue: not the thought itself, which outlived the empire and passed into Plato and Aristotle, but the specific edges that eroded — the naval-skill monopoly overtaken, the strategic judgment squandered after Pericles.

D09. Cultural Domain

Athens's self-image was explicit and proud: the school of Hellas, the freest and most open city, leader of the Greeks against Persia, the artistic and democratic center — the Funeral Oration is its purest statement. The war years were the height of its expression: the tragic theater, Aristophanes's comedy, and the Parthenon, finished on the eve of war and built partly with allied tribute — the empire's wealth turned to stone. The war strained that self-image hard. Athens called itself the liberator of Greece while its own allies, and Athenians too, called the empire a tyranny; the picture of the open, just city sat against plague, demagoguery, massacre, and defeat. Residue: the hollowing of the civic self-image as events undercut it — the distance between the Funeral Oration and the reality, the myth of Athenian rightness punctured even as the art reached its peak.

D10. Spiritual Domain

Civic religion ran through everything: Athena as patron, the Acropolis cults, sacrifice and omens before battle and Assembly, the great festivals — the Panathenaia, and the Dionysia where the plays were staged — and the oracles, though Delphi leaned toward Sparta. Religion shaped the war's turning points directly. The Sicilian Expedition was shadowed by the mutilation of sacred statues and an impiety scandal in 415 BCE, and its end was sealed when Nicias, a deeply pious commander, delayed the retreat from Syracuse after a lunar eclipse in 413 BCE on his seers' advice — and lost the army. The plague had already shaken the frame: with prayer and temple useless against it, reverence and law broke down together. Residue: the disenchantment the war bred — faith that the gods rewarded the pious shaken by plague, sacred treasures melted for the war, the moral flattening of Melos — a cynicism that fed the religious anxiety of the aftermath.
