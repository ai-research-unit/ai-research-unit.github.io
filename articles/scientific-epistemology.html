Four Criteria for Scientific Rigor

A Demarcation-and-Validity Framework
AI Research Unit

A scientific claim must do more than match observations. To count as a rigorous scientific contribution, it must satisfy a set of epistemic criteria that range from the minimal conditions of scientific legitimacy to the structural architecture of a mature theory. These four criteria form a simultaneous profile rather than a sequential filter: strength on one dimension does not compensate for failure on another, and a theory's overall standing is the composite of its performance across all four.

The four are not, however, perfectly symmetric. Two of them — falsifiability (Criterion 1) and operational isolation (Criterion 4) — function partly as enabling conditions: each carries a threshold below which the theory cannot be assessed at all, and above which it behaves like a graded dimension that trades off against the others. A theory whose terms are hopelessly blurred cannot have its predictions evaluated; a theory immune to all conceivable refutation offers nothing to evaluate. The other two — novel predictive risk (Criterion 2) and auxiliary resilience (Criterion 3) — are achievements assessed once those conditions are met. All four are still judged together; the asymmetry is only that 1 and 4 have a gate buried inside them, while 2 and 3 are graded all the way down.

The criteria are not orthogonal. They can pull against each other, and the tensions between them are often where the real analytical work begins. A theory can increase its falsifiability by committing its auxiliaries to precise, independently testable claims — but this makes it more brittle under anomaly, straining Criterion 3. Conversely, a theory can protect its core by keeping auxiliaries flexible — improving resilience but risking erosion of the falsifiability floor. A theory with precisely isolated variables may be confined to artificial experimental conditions, sacrificing the predictive reach that comes from engaging messier real-world phenomena. The framework is most useful not as a checklist but as a diagnostic instrument: mapping where a research program is strong, where it is weak, and where its criteria are in active tension with one another.

Full satisfaction of all four criteria is a retrospective achievement, not a precondition for ongoing inquiry. Partial profiles are the normal condition of productive science, and tension between criteria is often a sign of genuine empirical engagement rather than theoretical failure.

Criterion 1 — Methodological Falsifiability

The Threshold Condition

Falsifiability is the entry condition for empirical science: a claim must be vulnerable to contradiction by some conceivable observation. The line it draws is not a clean binary between "science" and "non-science" — that binary has not survived scrutiny — but a graded boundary, and the framework treats it as such. Logical falsifiability alone is not enough. A claim is scientifically meaningful only if its proponents specify in advance what evidence would refute it and commit to revising or abandoning the claim should that evidence appear.

Two complications deserve acknowledgment. First, falsifiability is a matter of degree rather than a clean binary: whether an observation counts as disconfirming depends on which auxiliary assumptions are held fixed, and there is rarely a single decisive test. Second, pseudoscientific systems often make superficially testable statements but protect themselves through reinterpretation, selective attention, or unfalsifiable escape clauses — satisfying the letter of falsifiability while violating its spirit.

Falsifiability is best understood as a floor with a gate at the bottom. Below a minimum, a theory is disqualified regardless of its performance on the other three criteria; above that minimum, falsifiability becomes a graded dimension assessed simultaneously with the rest, not a hurdle the others queue behind. And its logic does not stay confined to this criterion. The same test — does this claim expose itself to some possible refutation? — reappears as the connective tissue of the whole framework: it is what distinguishes a legitimate auxiliary from a patch in Criterion 3, and what distinguishes a genuine prediction from an overfitted rationalization in Criterion 2. Criterion 1 is less the first item on a list than the recursive standard applied to a theory's sub-components throughout.

Gold Standard. Newtonian mechanics predicts the trajectory of a projectile under fully specified initial conditions — mass, velocity, angle, gravitational field. Any reproducible deviation from the predicted path, under those exact conditions, falsifies the relevant application of the theory. The refutation conditions can be stated without invoking the anomaly-absorption machinery of Criterion 3.

Failure Mode. "An invisible, undetectable force pushes objects downward." No possible observation could disconfirm it.

Criterion 2 — Novel Predictive Risk

The Test of Explanatory Power

A scientific claim must generate expectations about the unknown. Explanation after the fact is insufficient; a theory must take empirical risks — it must forbid something, so that the world has a chance to prove it wrong.

Predictive success comes in two forms:

  • Tier 1 — Novel Prediction: forecasting a phenomenon before it is observed.
  • Tier 2 — Retrodictive Resolution: explaining a longstanding anomaly that previous frameworks could not account for.

These tiers are not strictly ranked. Retrodiction of a well-documented, stubbornly resistant anomaly can be epistemically stronger than a novel prediction of an unconfirmed phenomenon, precisely because the anomaly is already fixed. What matters in both cases is the same: that the theory constrained the prediction space independently of the outcome it is credited with.

It is tempting to cash this out historically — the theory counts only if it was "built on separate grounds," before or apart from the anomaly. But the historical story is a proxy, and a fallible one, for something structural. What actually does the epistemic work is whether the theory had any free parameters available to absorb the anomaly. A theory with adjustable parameters can be tuned to fit a known result no matter when its authors first encountered that result; a theory with none earns full credit even if its authors were staring speechlessly at the anomaly the whole time. The deep diagnostic is therefore not "when was it built?" but "could it have been made to fit either way?" Historical independence matters only because it is usually good evidence of parametric independence — not in its own right.

This is what separates genuine retrodiction from post-hoc rationalization. A theory with enough free parameters to reproduce a known anomaly offers no retrodictive evidence at all, however precisely it fits. Statisticians call the limiting case overfitting: a model with enough adjustable parameters can match any finite dataset exactly while capturing little or none of its underlying structure, and will fail on genuinely new input. Post-hoc rationalization is overfitting applied to explanation — the appearance of fit without the substance of understanding. The principle underwriting the whole diagnostic is parsimony: what counts in a theory's favor is the explanatory content it delivers relative to the free parameters it spends, and a theory that spends a parameter for every fact it accommodates has, in the relevant sense, explained nothing.

Two clarifications about scope. First, novelty (or risk) is not the same as breadth. A single, narrow, but severe prediction — one number whose every other value the theory forbids — can be more probative than a wide explanatory reach that risks nothing. Breadth and unification are genuine additional virtues, but they are not what this criterion measures, and a theory can score high here on the strength of one well-aimed risk. Second, note the tension with Criterion 4: a theory that does achieve broad reach across diverse, real-world phenomena often does so by tolerating some conceptual looseness in its variables. Gains in reach can come at a cost to precision. This is not a defect to be eliminated but a trade-off to be managed and named.

Gold Standard (Tier 1). Mendeleev predicted the properties of undiscovered elements from gaps in the periodic table — specific, checkable values, stated before the elements were found.

Gold Standard (Tier 2). General Relativity accounted for Mercury's anomalous orbital precession, which Newtonian mechanics could not. The decisive feature was structural: GR's field equations contained no free parameter that could be dialed to the observed precession. The derivation produced one specific number, take it or leave it. That the theory was also developed on independent grounds — the equivalence principle, the geometry of spacetime — is corroborating, but it is the absence of an adjustable parameter, not the biography of the theory, that makes the retrodiction count.

Gold Standard (spanning both, outside the laboratory). Plate tectonics retrodicted the jigsaw fit of the continents and the matching fossil distributions across oceans, and predicted, in advance, the symmetric magnetic striping of the seafloor on either side of mid-ocean ridges — later confirmed. None of this was producible in a controlled experiment; the theory took its risks in the field. It is a reminder that the historical and observational sciences can meet this criterion as fully as physics.

Criterion 3 — Auxiliary Resilience

The Measure of Structural Strength

All theories encounter anomalies. Weak theories collapse or are abandoned prematurely; strong theories distinguish between their core principles and their auxiliary assumptions. When an anomaly appears, a resilient theory can adjust or discard an auxiliary without damaging its central explanatory engine.

The danger is degenerative ad hoc patching — adding untestable, hyper-specific assumptions for no reason except to shield the theory from contradiction. When this happens, the theory stops generating new predictions and becomes purely self-protective. The diagnostic, which is Criterion 1 applied recursively, is whether each adjustment carries independent empirical consequences of its own. An auxiliary that can be tested separately from the anomaly that prompted it is a legitimate refinement; one that does nothing except absorb the anomaly is a patch.

There is a complication the framework must not paper over: the line between core and auxiliary is not given in advance. It is itself a theoretical commitment, and therefore itself revisable — which makes it a second site of potential abuse. A degenerating program can defend itself not only by patching auxiliaries but by redrawing the boundary: quietly demoting a once-central claim to "auxiliary" so it can be discarded, or promoting an ad hoc patch toward the "core" so it looks principled. Any such redrawing must face the same recursive test as the auxiliaries themselves — does the new partition expose the theory to consequences it did not face before, or does it merely relocate the anomaly out of harm's way? Retrospective judgments are especially vulnerable here: we tend to call exactly those assumptions "auxiliary" that the successful theories turned out to shed.

The tension with Criterion 1 is structural and worth naming directly. A theory that achieves high resilience by maintaining many flexible auxiliaries — absorbing anomalies without collapsing — may be doing so at the cost of its falsifiability floor. String theory is the canonical live case: its resilience is partly a function of the same flexibility that makes decisive disconfirmation practically unreachable. Resilience bought at the price of unfalsifiability is not a strength; it is the failure mode of Criterion 1 wearing the costume of Criterion 3. The two must be assessed jointly to catch the maneuver.

Gold Standard. The failure to detect the luminiferous aether did not destroy wave optics. Physicists discarded the assumption of a physical medium while preserving the core wave equations — by then part of Maxwell's electromagnetism — which Einstein in turn took as the invariant foundation of special relativity. Two features make this a clean case rather than a lucky one: the discarded assumption had been independently testable — the Michelson–Morley apparatus was built precisely to probe it — and the preserved core was not logically implicated in its failure. It is worth admitting that the "medium" looks more obviously auxiliary in hindsight than it did at the time, when it was close to constitutive of the wave picture; which is exactly why the independent testability of the discarded piece, not its retrospective label, is what carries the verdict.

Failure Mode. The Ptolemaic system survived anomalies by adding epicycles upon epicycles, becoming an ad hoc patchwork that protected a false core. Each epicycle was introduced solely to capture a specific deviation and generated no independent predictions — the partition was redrawn endlessly, and never once exposed the system to new risk.

Criterion 4 — Operational Isolation

The Requirement of Clear Variables

A mature scientific claim must satisfy two related but distinct demands. First, its concepts must be defined precisely enough to be measured — terms must have operational criteria that let independent investigators apply them consistently. Second, its key variables must be isolable enough to be varied separately, so that causal claims can be tested against genuine controls.

These demands can come apart. Temperature and pressure are each precisely operationalized yet not fully isolable in many real systems; managing that requires explicit experimental design. The more basic failure is the first: if concepts are vague, overlapping, or ambiguously defined, empirical tests become uninterpretable, and the theory becomes immune to failure through semantic flexibility — definitions quietly shift after the fact to fit inconvenient results.

The tension with Criterion 2 runs in both directions. Tight operational isolation enables clean testing but often restricts a theory to artificial laboratory conditions, limiting reach. Conversely, a theory ambitious enough to predict behavior across complex natural systems — ecology, climate, economics — will typically struggle to isolate its variables cleanly. The remedy outside the laboratory is not physical isolation but its functional substitutes: statistical control, instrumental variables, natural experiments. Plate tectonics illustrates the point again — its variables were never isolable on a bench, yet its concepts were sharp enough, and its controls (the ocean floor on either side of a ridge) natural enough, to permit decisive tests. Acknowledging this is not a concession to imprecision; it is a condition of applying the framework honestly to sciences that operate outside the laboratory.

Gold Standard. The Standard Model isolates particles by exact quantum numbers, allowing unambiguous identification even in probabilistic contexts. Distinct particles cannot be redefined as one another to salvage a failed prediction. Particles can, of course, transform into one another in nature — quarks change flavor in weak decays, neutrinos oscillate between flavors — but the mixing rules and their measured parameters are mathematically rigid, and the quantum numbers that identify each particle are never redefined after the fact to absorb a discrepancy.

Failure Mode. Early psychoanalytic constructs such as "ego" and "subconscious" lacked discriminant validity — the property that two genuinely distinct concepts must yield divergent predictions in at least some observable circumstances. With no agreed measurement criteria, the constructs could overlap completely: the same action could be attributed at once to the ego and to a subconscious defense without contradiction. No observation could ever adjudicate between them. Variables that cannot in principle come apart cannot in principle be tested. This is the precise failure Criterion 4 is built to catch — not mere vagueness, but the structural impossibility of isolation.

A Note on Scope

These four criteria concern a theory's relationship to its own evidence — whether it can be refuted, whether it predicts, whether it withstands stress, whether it can be cleanly tested. They are deliberately silent on a fifth virtue that some accounts treat as central: consilience, or external coherence — the degree to which a theory connects to and is constrained by independently established results elsewhere in science. A claim can score well on all four criteria while sitting in near-total isolation from the rest of established knowledge, and most working scientists would count that isolation against it. Whether consilience belongs as a fifth criterion or as a background constraint on the other four is left open here; it is flagged so that its absence is a deliberate choice rather than an oversight.

The Demarcation-and-Validity Matrix

(Demarcation here means a graded boundary, not a binary verdict.)

Criterion 1 — Falsifiable

  • Function: Sets the minimum condition of scientific legitimacy; its logic recurs throughout the framework, inside both Criterion 2 and Criterion 3.
  • Indicator: Proponents specify in advance what evidence would refute the claim; falsifiability is treated as a matter of degree, with a disqualifying floor.
  • Failure Mode: Pseudoscience — disconfirming data is reinterpreted away; escape clauses are added after the fact.
  • Key Tension: High resilience (Criterion 3) bought through auxiliary flexibility can erode the falsifiability floor; assess the two jointly.

Criterion 2 — Predictive (Novel and Risky)

  • Function: Demonstrates explanatory power by taking empirical risk.
  • Indicator: Produces novel predictions, or resolves prior anomalies, with no free parameter available to fit the outcome it is credited with.
  • Failure Mode: Post-hoc rationalization — enough free parameters to reproduce the result; retrodiction indistinguishable from overfitting.
  • Key Tension: Broad scope across real-world phenomena often requires tolerating looseness in variable definition (Criterion 4). Note also that risk and breadth are distinct virtues; this criterion measures the former.

Criterion 3 — Resilient

  • Function: Measures durability under empirical stress.
  • Indicator: Adjusts auxiliaries while preserving the core; each adjustment carries independent empirical consequences (Criterion 1 applied recursively); any redrawing of the core/auxiliary line is itself subjected to that test.
  • Failure Mode: Ad hoc patching — auxiliary additions, or quiet boundary redrawings, that absorb anomalies without generating new exposure.
  • Key Tension: Resilience through flexible auxiliaries can be Criterion 1 failure in disguise; assess jointly with falsifiability.

Criterion 4 — Isolating

  • Function: Ensures clarity and testability.
  • Indicator: Concepts are operationally defined; key variables are independent enough to permit controlled testing — whether by physical isolation or by its functional substitutes outside the laboratory.
  • Failure Mode: Conceptual blur — definitions shift to fit convenience; concepts cannot be cleanly disentangled, even in principle.
  • Key Tension: Tight operational isolation may confine a theory to laboratory conditions, limiting the predictive reach of Criterion 2.
